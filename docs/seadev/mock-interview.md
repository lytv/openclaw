### Question 2.2: How do you prioritize when you have multiple projects?

**Pause and think...**

<details>
<summary>Suggested Answer</summary>

> "When managing multiple projects, I use a simple prioritization framework:
>
> **Step 1: List everything** - Write down all tasks and deadlines
>
> **Step 2: Categorize by urgency and impact**
> - **Urgent + High Impact:** Do immediately
> - **Urgent + Low Impact:** Delegate or quick complete
> - **Not Urgent + High Impact:** Schedule dedicated time
> - **Not Urgent + Low Impact:** Do later or eliminate
>
> **Step 3: Apply the 2-minute rule** - If something takes less than 2 minutes, do it now
>
> **Step 4: Communicate proactively** - If deadlines conflict, I inform stakeholders early rather than waiting until the last minute
>
> **Example:** Last month, I had 3 projects due simultaneously. I communicated with all stakeholders, negotiated one deadline by 3 days (they agreed), and delivered all three on time by prioritizing focused work blocks.
>
> The key is being transparent about capacity and not overcommitting."

### Question 2.3: How do you handle tight deadlines?

**Pause and think...**

<details>
<summary>Suggested Answer</summary>

> "I approach tight deadlines with a structured approach:
>
> **First, assess reality.** Is this deadline truly fixed, or can it be negotiated? I ask: 'What happens if we miss this?' Sometimes there's flexibility I don't know about.
>
> **Second, break it down.** I create a task breakdown and identify the critical path - what must happen first, what can happen in parallel.
>
> **Third, communicate early.** If I see a risk of missing the deadline, I raise it immediately with options: 'We can deliver X by Friday or Y by Wednesday - which do you prefer?'
>
> **Fourth, focus ruthlessly.** I eliminate non-essential tasks and get buy-in from stakeholders on scope.
>
> **Example:** Last year, I had a client request that came in with a 48-hour turnaround. I worked with my team to deliver a comprehensive response in 36 hours by focusing on their core need and scheduling the 'nice-to-haves' for follow-up. The client was impressed with the speed.
>
> The key is proactive communication and scope management, not just working harder."

**Length:** 1.5-2 minutes

</details>

## 4. Round 3: Situational & Scenario

### Scenario 3.1: The Angry Client

**Situation:**
"A key ShedPro client calls you furious because their Google Ads have been paused for 3 days and they lost significant leads. The US Account Manager is unavailable. What do you do?"

**Pause and think...**

<details>
<summary>Suggested Answer</summary>

> "This is a high-priority situation that needs immediate action. Here's what I would do:
>
> **Immediate (First 30 minutes):**
> 1. **Acknowledge the client's frustration** - 'I understand how critical this is and I'm taking ownership right now'
> 2. **Gather basic information** - Which Google Ads account? When did it start? Any error messages?
> 3. **Check our internal systems** - Is there a ticket? Did our team make a change?
>
> **Investigation (Next 2 hours):**
> 4. **Contact the product/operations team** - Ask if they're aware of the issue
> 5. **Check Google's side** - Look for payment issues, policy violations, or account holds
> 6. **Escalate to my manager** - Inform them of the situation even if the US AM is unavailable
>
> **Resolution:**
> 7. **Provide updates every 2 hours** - Even if no solution yet, the client needs to know I'm working on it
> 8. **Once resolved, do a post-mortem** - How did this happen? How do we prevent it?
>
> **Throughout:** Maintain a calm, solution-oriented tone. The client is upset because their business is affected - I need to focus on fixing the business impact, not defending the company."

**Length:** 2-3 minutes

**Key traits shown:** Ownership, urgency, communication, escalation, problem-solving

</details>

### Scenario 3.2: Competing Priorities

**Situation:**
"You have three tasks due this week:
1. A new client onboarding (high revenue impact, deadline Friday)
2. A client training session (scheduled for Wednesday)
3. Documentation update for the product team (low urgency)

But on Monday morning, your manager adds an urgent request: prepare a presentation for a potential new client (due Wednesday).

How do you handle this?"

**Pause and think...**

<details>
<summary>Suggested Answer</summary>

> "This is a classic prioritization challenge. Here's how I would approach it:
>
> **Step 1: Assess all deadlines and impacts**
> - New client onboarding: Friday = 4 days, high revenue = priority
> - Training session: Wednesday = 2 days, already scheduled = hard commitment
> - Documentation: Low urgency, flexible
> - New presentation: Wednesday = 2 days, potential revenue
>
> **Step 2: Identify options**
> - Can documentation be delayed further? Yes, it's low urgency
> - Can training be moved? Probably not, clients expect it
> - Can onboarding start later? Only if client agrees
> - Can I delegate any of this?
>
> **Step 3: Communicate proactively**
> I would talk to my manager: 'I want to make sure this presentation gets the attention it deserves. I have the training on Wednesday and onboarding due Friday. Could we either A) move documentation to next week to free up time, or B) get help from [colleague] on part of the onboarding?'
>
> **Step 4: Execute with focus**
> Once the plan is set, I would block focused time on my calendar and eliminate distractions during those blocks.
>
> **Key principle:** Never just accept more work without discussing capacity. Proactive communication prevents burnout and missed deadlines."

**Length:** 2 minutes

**Key traits shown:** Prioritization framework, communication, collaboration

</details>

### Scenario 3.4: Cross-Team Conflict

**Situation:**
"The US Account Manager tells you a client needs a feature by Friday. The product team says it will take 3 weeks and isn't planned. The client is important to the company. What do you do?"

**Pause and think...**

<details>
<summary>Suggested Answer</summary>

> "This is a delicate situation requiring diplomacy and clarity. Here's my approach:
>
> **Step 1: Don't promise anything**
> I wouldn't tell the client 'yes' or the product team 'no' until I understand all perspectives.
>
> **Step 2: Understand both sides**
> - Talk to the product team: 'What's the technical reality? Is there any way to accelerate?'
> - Talk to the Account Manager: 'Why does the client need this by Friday? What happens if it's delayed?'
>
> **Step 3: Find middle ground**
> Possible solutions:
> - Can we deliver a partial solution by Friday that addresses the core need?
> - Can the client use a workaround for 3 weeks?
> - Is this a genuine business need or a 'nice to have'?
>
> **Step 4: Communicate transparently**
> I would facilitate a call or write a clear email: 'After discussing with both teams, here's what we can do: [Option A, Option B]. What works best for you?'
>
> **Step 5: Document and escalate if needed**
> If the Account Manager and product team fundamentally disagree, I would escalate to the Project Manager/Product Director with all context.
>
> **Core principle:** Be the bridge, not the barrier. My job is to help both sides understand each other, not pick sides."

**Length:** 2-3 minutes

**Key traits shown:** Diplomacy, communication, problem-solving

</details>

## 6. Your Questions to Ask

### Questions You SHOULD Ask

| Question | Why It Matters |
|----------|----------------|
| "What does success look like in this role after 90 days?" | Shows results-orientation |
| "How does this role collaborate with the US Account Manager?" | Shows cross-functional awareness |
| "What are the biggest challenges the team is facing right now?" | Shows problem-solving mindset |
| "How do you measure performance in this position?" | Shows metrics-focus |
| "What's the typical career path from here?" | Shows long-term interest |
| "Can you tell me more about the company's community involvement?" | Shows values alignment |
| "What does a typical day or week look like in this role?" | Practical understanding |

### Questions to AVOID

- "How much does this position pay?" (Wait for them to bring up salary)
- "What are the hours?" (Already in job description)

## 7. Answer Key & Tips

### Checklist for Strong Answers

| Element | Strong Answer | Weak Answer |
|---------|---------------|-------------|
| **Specificity** | Uses real examples, numbers | Generic statements |
| **STAR Structure** | Situation → Task → Action → Result | Rambling, no structure |
| **Relevance** | Connects to the role | Irrelevant details |
| **Brevity** | 1-3 minutes | Too long or too short |
| **Enthusiasm** | Shows genuine interest | Flat, monotone |

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Why It's Bad | Fix |
|---------|--------------|-----|
| "I don't have experience with that" | Too negative | "I haven't used [X] specifically, but I've used [Y] which has similar functionality" |
| "I think..." | Uncertain | "I believe..." or "Based on my experience..." |
| "I did everything" | Not collaborative | "I led the team while [colleague] handled..." |
| "My boss was terrible" | Negative | "The team dynamics were challenging, and I learned..." |
| "I don't know" (too often) | No curiosity | "I'm not familiar with that specifically, but I'd want to learn by..." |

### Body Language Tips

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Make eye contact (camera in virtual) | Look away or at notes constantly |
| Sit up straight | Slouch or lean back |
| Nod to show understanding | Fidget or touch your face |
| Smile genuinely | Force unnatural expressions |
| Use hand gestures (subtle) | Wave hands frantically |
